# Motivation and Fulfillment (Tynan Sylvester)

From "Designing Games: A Guide to Engineering Experiences"

## Core Insight

**Dopamine is NOT pleasure—it's motivation.** We can want something without liking it, or like something without wanting it.

- Drug addicts want their drug more and more while liking it less
- Players can find themselves unable to stop playing long after becoming bored
- The prehistoric brain has no defense against carefully tuned reinforcement schedules

## Rewards Anticipation

The main way we generate dopamine motivation is by creating **anticipation of rewards**.

**Virtual rewards work** because the brain evolved without modern games—it can't distinguish real rewards from virtual ones:
- Experience points, in-game money, equipment
- Story content (cutscenes, audio logs)
- Records of achievement (points, trophies)

## Reinforcement Schedules

**Definition:** A system of rules that defines when rewards are given.

### Fixed Ratio
Rewards at fixed ratio to actions (e.g., gold every 10th orc).

**Problem:** Poor motivator. Creates long inactivity periods followed by activity surges. Easy for player to walk away.

### Variable Ratio
Randomized ratio (e.g., 10% chance of reward per orc).

**Power:** Most powerful simple schedule. Player always has chance at reward on next action, so activity stays high. This is why slot machines work.

### Superimposed Schedules

> "The power of reinforcement schedules isn't in any one schedule—it is in superimposing them so that there is always at least one that is producing high motivation."

**"Grinding RPG" technique:**
- Gold for every 10th chest
- Diamond for every 10th rock mined
- Arrow for every 10th goblin killed
- Player shifts focus between activities, never hitting a motivation gap

**"One more turn syndrome" (Civilization):** 30+ superimposed schedules running simultaneously. Player is never more than one turn from some reward.

### Emergent Schedules

Most reinforcement schedules aren't explicitly designed—they emerge from game systems.

**Example:** Deathmatch kill counts
- **Consistent outcomes** (fixed ratio feel) → monotonous, players leave
- **Variable outcomes** (variable ratio feel) → any match could be the 30-kill streak, players stay

## Extrinsic vs Intrinsic Motivation

### The Displacement Effect

**Extrinsic rewards can displace and even destroy intrinsic fulfillment.**

Making an orc drop gold reduces players' natural desire to fight him.

**Deci's Study:**
- Group A: Given rewards for intrinsically fun task, then rewards removed → lost interest
- Group B: Never rewarded → kept happily working

**Other findings:**
- Children create less interesting art when offered rewards
- Chess players solve fewer problems in free time after being paid
- Student poets write less interesting poems when reminded writers can make money

### When Extrinsic Works

- **Boring tasks:** No intrinsic motivation to displace
- **Aligned rewards:** Reward what player already wants to do

### Rewards Alignment

> "REWARDS ALIGNMENT is how closely the activities encouraged by a reward system resemble those the player would have engaged in without it."

**Good alignment (Skate 3):**
- Scoring notices every turn, flip, grind, jump
- Counts milliseconds of air time, centimeters of grind distance
- Actions rewarded ≈ actions player would naturally do
- Result: High scores feel desirable and enjoyable

**Bad alignment:**
- Reward only for jumping through floating hoops
- Players ignore everything else
- Life sucked out of elegant mechanics

**Non-standard goals need special modes:**
- Races (different win condition)
- Copy another skater's sequence
- Hall of Meat: break as many bones as possible

## Player's Remorse

**Definition:** Appears after player spends time on a game that motivates but does not fulfill.

### The Ethical Spectrum

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Good** | Well-aligned rewards + meaningful core experience |
| **Mixed** | Alternates compulsion and fulfillment (hours of grind, then exciting boss) |
| **Compulsion Machine** | Every decision maximizes motivation, no fulfillment attempt |

**Cow Clicker (parody):** Naked fixed interval schedule with no content. Thousands played anyway. "Dopamine is a hell of a brain chemical."

### Designer's Choice

> "I think that any designer who cares enough to master the craft wants to do more than build Skinner boxes."

Over time, players learn to avoid player's remorse. They will still want what they've always wanted: **new ideas, new friends, and new experiences**.

## Key Mantras

- "We can want something without liking it, or like something without wanting it."
- "The power of reinforcement schedules isn't in any one schedule—it is in superimposing them."
- "Extrinsic rewards can displace and even destroy the intrinsic fulfillment of play."
- "You should only reward things that the player already wants to do."
