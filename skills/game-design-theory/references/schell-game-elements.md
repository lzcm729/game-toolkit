# Game Elements & Theme (Jesse Schell)

From "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses" (3rd Edition)

## Core Concept

**A game is a problem-solving activity, approached with a playful attitude.** Every game consists of four basic elements that must work in harmony toward a unified theme.

---

## What Is a Game?

### Ten Qualities of Games

1. Games are **entered willfully**
2. Games have **goals**
3. Games have **conflict**
4. Games have **rules**
5. Games can be **won and lost**
6. Games are **interactive**
7. Games have **challenge**
8. Games can create their own **internal value**
9. Games **engage** players
10. Games are **closed, formal systems**

### Key Definitions

> **Play** is manipulation that indulges curiosity.

> **Fun** is pleasure with surprises.

> **A game** is a problem-solving activity, approached with a playful attitude.

### Endogenous Value

Things that have value inside the game have value **only** inside the game. The more compelling a game, the greater the endogenous value it creates.

**Test:** If players pursue in-game rewards eagerly, your game has strong endogenous value. If they ignore optional rewards, it's weak.

## The Elemental Tetrad

```
        AESTHETICS
           /\
          /  \
         /    \
    STORY ---- MECHANICS
         \    /
          \  /
           \/
       TECHNOLOGY
```

### 1. Mechanics
**Rules and procedures** - What players can and cannot do, what happens when they try.

- Makes a game a game (unlike passive media)
- Must be supported by technology
- Emphasized by aesthetics
- Given meaning by story

### 2. Story
**Sequence of events** - May be linear/prescripted or branching/emergent.

- Mechanics should strengthen story
- Aesthetics reinforce story ideas
- Technology enables story delivery

### 3. Aesthetics
**Look, sound, smell, taste, feel** - Most direct relationship to player experience.

- Technology enables aesthetics
- Must feel consistent with mechanics
- Should pace well with story

### 4. Technology
**Materials and interactions** that make the game possible (not just "high tech").

- Enables certain things, prohibits others
- Medium for aesthetics, mechanics, story
- Examples: paper/pencil, plastic chits, software

### Visibility Gradient

| Least Visible | → | Most Visible |
|--------------|---|--------------|
| Technology | Mechanics/Story | Aesthetics |

### Key Insight

> "None of the elements are more important than the others... Each one powerfully influences each of the others."

## Theme & Unification

### Why Theme Matters

- Focuses design toward a single goal
- Provides easy method for decisions: If it reinforces theme, keep it; if not, cut it
- Creates stronger, more resonant experiences

### Two Steps to Using Theme

1. **Figure out what your theme is**
2. **Use every means possible to reinforce that theme**

### Types of Themes

**Experience-Based Themes:**
- About delivering a certain essential experience
- Example: "The fantasy of being a pirate"

**Truth-Based Themes:**
- Express a deep human truth
- Example: "Love is stronger than death" (Titanic)
- Often hidden, felt rather than stated

### Pirates of the Caribbean Example

Theme: "The fantasy of being a pirate"

Every design decision reinforced this:
- CAVE shape designed for pirate ship
- Stereoscopic 3D for sense of being at sea
- Motion platform felt like rocking boat
- Ship's wheel and metal cannons (not joysticks)
- Theme music from the ride
- 10-speaker audio for all-around immersion
- Flying skeletons steal treasure (makes defense meaningful)
- Air conditioning vents blow from front (sea breeze)
- Even staff called 3D glasses "Eyes of Bluebeard"

## Resonance

### What Makes Themes Powerful

Resonant themes tap into deep human fantasies and truths:
- Universal desires (freedom, power, belonging)
- Deep personal truths we rarely express
- Fantasy fulfillment everyone has experienced

### Examples of Resonant Themes

| Game/Story | Resonant Theme |
|-----------|----------------|
| Pirates of the Caribbean | Freedom from obligations |
| Titanic | Love is stronger than death |
| Hercules | Virtue can defeat death |
| Toontown Online | Play vs. Work (freedom vs. slavery) |

### Finding Resonance

> "You can never tell which themes are resonant just through logic—you have to feel the resonance, deep inside yourself."

## Holographic Design

### Skin and Skeleton

- **Skin** = The player's experience
- **Skeleton** = The elements that make up the game

**The skill:** See both simultaneously. Understand which elements cause which experiences.

### The Danger

> "Some designers, thinking constantly about the detailed internal workings of games, forget about the player experience."

Focus only on skin → Can't understand why things work
Focus only on skeleton → Beautiful in theory, horrible in practice

## Key Lenses

### #9 The Lens of the Elemental Tetrad
- Is my game using all four types of elements?
- Could design be improved by enhancing elements in one or more categories?
- Are the four elements in harmony, working toward a common theme?

### #10 The Lens of Holographic Design
- What elements make the experience enjoyable?
- What elements detract from the experience?
- How can I change game elements to improve the experience?

### #11 The Lens of Unification
- What is my theme?
- Am I using every means possible to reinforce that theme?

### #12 The Lens of Resonance
- What about my game feels powerful and special?
- When I describe my game, what ideas get people really excited?
- What instincts am I following about how this game should be?

## Practical Application

### Essential Experience Process

1. Identify the experience you want players to have
2. Determine what is **essential** to that experience
3. Find ways to capture that essence in your game
4. You don't need to perfectly replicate reality

### Example: Snowball Fight Game

Essential elements might be:
- Feeling of cold
- Excitement of competition
- Social chaos
- Physical movement

Non-essential:
- Specific clothing
- Exact location details
- Minor sensory details

**Capture essence:** Use artwork (puffs of breath), sound (whistling wind), mechanics (gloves on/off affecting snowball quality).
