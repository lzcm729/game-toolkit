# Elegance (Tynan Sylvester)

From "Designing Games: A Guide to Engineering Experiences"

## Core Definition

**Elegance:** Maximizing emotional power and variety of play experiences while minimizing players' comprehension burden and developer effort.

> "Every game mechanic has a price tag. It costs design effort, computing resources, player attention. Good design maximizes benefit while minimizing cost."

## Elegance from Emergence

**Emergence:** When simple mechanics interact to create complex situations.

**Example:** Checkers has few simple rules but generates endless variety of games—long struggles, quick sweeps, tactical upsets, important lessons. Cost? A few minutes of instructions.

### Mechanics Multiply, Not Add

```
Shooting alone → few experiences (hit/miss)
Looking alone → few experiences (scenic views)
Shooting + Looking → multiplication of experiences (aiming challenges, target prioritization)
Shooting + Looking + Moving → billions of experiences (entire FPS genre)
```

**Key insight:** Elegance happens when mechanics interact in complex, nonobvious ways. But this same complexity makes elegant design very difficult.

## Smelling Elegance (Heuristics)

### 1. Mechanics that interact with many others

Count interactions. Can a spell interact with other spells, party members, adversaries, environment, morality system, narrative, player stats?

### 2. Simple mechanics

> "The most elegant-smelling mechanics are so stupidly simple that they seem downright obvious after you hear them."

Find designs you can write on a cocktail napkin.

### 3. Mechanics usable in multiple ways

**Example:** Resident Evil guns are both offensive AND defensive (shoot to slow zombies, not just kill). This creates trade-offs between defensive and offensive needs.

### 4. Mechanics that don't overlap roles

If two mechanics do the same thing, you're paying the cost for two to do the work of one. Seek mechanics that introduce **new** strategies, not variations.

### 5. Reuse established conventions

Use genre-standard controls. Give villains pointy moustaches. Include nothing unique without good reason—unique things cost extra effort to understand.

### 6. Similar scale to existing mechanics

**Magic: The Gathering:** 7 cards, 20 life, 3-10 mana, 2-12 creatures, power/toughness 1-8. Close scaling allows natural conversions in all directions without fiddly math.

**Bad example:** Player on foot + fighter jet = two separate games. The jet's 1000-pound bombs make foot movement meaningless.

### 7. Mechanics reused many times

A mechanic used once is a gimmick. A mechanic used 100,000 times can be elegant. Look for million-repetition mechanics.

### 8. No content restrictions

**Bad:** 20-foot jump requires every level to block escape routes. Hidden cost spreads over years.

**Good:** Mechanics that work with existing content.

### 9. Full expressiveness of interface

Analog joystick can sense exact angle, not just direction. But the full spectrum should be useful to experts while ignorable by novices.

## Case Study: Predator vs Hellion (StarCraft II)

| Aspect | Predator | Hellion |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Attack Shape | Circle around self | Line projecting outward |
| Attack Frequency | 1 second | 2.5 seconds |
| Use Cases | Be surrounded, attack groups | Line up enemies, shoot and scoot |
| Synergies | Can't use cover or allies | Works behind walls, ledges, allies |
| Player Skill Expression | Low (throw in and watch) | High (movement between shots) |

**Result:** Hellion generates far more challenges, tactics, and situations. Same complexity to understand, vastly more elegant.

> "Elegance doesn't come from flashy gimmicks or exciting design pitches. It comes from simple, workmanlike designs that flower into a million experiences."

## Key Mantras

- "We don't create games. We find them like a sculptor finds the statue in the marble—by taking away the excess."
- "Unique things take extra effort to understand. Real originality comes from changing fundamentals, not surface details."
- "What matters is the depth and richness of the possibility space that emerges during play."
