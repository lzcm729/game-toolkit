# Experience Design (Jesse Schell)

From "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses" (3rd Edition)

## Core Concept

**The game is not the experience.** The game is a structure that creates experiences in players' minds. Designers must understand psychology, needs, and motivation to craft compelling experiences.

---

## The Player's Mind

### Mental Models

Our brains don't deal with reality directly - they create simplified **mental models**:
- We filter reality through senses and minds
- What we experience is an illusion, a simulation
- Games are "predigested" models - simpler than reality, easier to absorb
- This is why games feel relaxing - less cognitive load than real world

**Implication for design:** Games that match how our brain naturally models things feel more intuitive.

### Four Mental Abilities Enabling Gameplay

1. **Modeling** - Creating simplified internal representations
2. **Focus** - Selective attention on what matters
3. **Empathy** - Projecting ourselves into others' situations
4. **Imagination** - Filling in gaps and manipulating mental models

## Flow State (Csikszentmihalyi)

### Requirements for Flow
- **Clear goals** - Know what you're trying to achieve
- **No distractions** - Environment supports focus
- **Direct feedback** - Know immediately how actions affect outcomes
- **Continuously challenging** - Not too easy, not too hard

### The Flow Channel

```
    High  |     Anxiety Zone
Challenge |   /
          |  /  FLOW CHANNEL
          | /
    Low   |    Boredom Zone
          +-------------------
             Low    High
                Skill
```

### Tense and Release Pattern

Better than straight progression through flow channel:
1. Increase challenge
2. Reward with more power
3. Brief easy period
4. Challenge ramps up again

> "Too much tension, and we wear out. Too much relaxation, and we grow bored."

## Player Needs

### Maslow's Hierarchy Applied to Games

| Level | Need | Game Examples |
|-------|------|---------------|
| 5 | Self-actualization | Creative expression, building |
| 4 | Self-esteem | Achievement, mastery |
| 3 | Love/belonging | Multiplayer, community |
| 2 | Safety | Fantasy contexts (shelter building) |
| 1 | Physiological | - |

**Insight:** Multiplayer games fulfill more basic needs than single-player, explaining their staying power.

### Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

Three core mental needs:
1. **Competence** - "I need to feel good at something"
2. **Autonomy** - "I need freedom to do things my way"
3. **Relatedness** - "I need to connect with others"

Games naturally fulfill all three:
- Designed to make you feel mastery
- Give freedom in how you play
- Often played with others

## Motivation

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic

| Type | Source | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| External | Payment | Playing for prizes |
| Introjected | Commitment | "Because I said I would" |
| Identified | Values | "Because I think it's important" |
| Integrated | Identity | "Because I'm that kind of person" |
| Intrinsic | Self | "Because I enjoy it" |

**Warning:** Adding extrinsic rewards can diminish intrinsic motivation. In studies, paid children stopped drawing when payment stopped; unpaid children kept drawing voluntarily.

### Wanna vs. Hafta

> "Whoever must play cannot play."

- The less obligated you feel, the more it feels like play
- Work = servitude; Play = freedom
- Games should feel like "wanna," not "hafta"

## Player Types & Pleasures

### LeBlanc's Eight Game Pleasures

1. **Sensation** - Sensory pleasure (visuals, sounds, touch)
2. **Fantasy** - Imagining yourself as something you're not
3. **Narrative** - Dramatic unfolding of events
4. **Challenge** - Problem solving at the core
5. **Fellowship** - Friendship, cooperation, community
6. **Discovery** - Seeking and finding new things
7. **Expression** - Creating and showing yourself
8. **Submission** - Entering the magic circle, escaping reality

### Bartle's Player Types

| Type | Primary Pleasure | Interest |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| Achievers | Challenge | Acting on the world |
| Explorers | Discovery | Interacting with the world |
| Socializers | Fellowship | Interacting with players |
| Killers | Competition/Dominance | Acting on players |

### Additional Pleasures to Consider

- **Anticipation** - Waiting for known pleasure
- **Completion** - Finishing something
- **Schadenfreude** - Delight in others' misfortune
- **Gift giving** - Making others happy
- **Humor** - Unexpected connections
- **Possibility** - Having many choices
- **Pride (Naches)** - Satisfaction in accomplishment
- **Surprise** - The unexpected
- **Thrill** - Fear minus death
- **Fiero** - Triumph over adversity
- **Wonder** - Awe and amazement

## Understanding Your Player

### Demographics to Consider

- Age (different needs at different life stages)
- Gender (different preferences, though stereotypes)
- Culture (different attitudes toward chance, competition)

### Gender Stereotypes (Use Thoughtfully)

**Male preferences:**
- Mastery, competition
- Destruction
- Spatial reasoning
- Trial and error learning

**Female preferences:**
- Emotional depth
- Real-world connection
- Nurturing mechanics
- Verbal/dialog elements
- Learning by example

### The Player Lens Questions

- In general, what do they like?
- What don't they like? Why?
- What do they expect to see in a game?
- If I were in their place, what would I want to see?
- What would they like or dislike about my game in particular?

## Key Lenses

### #19 The Lens of the Player
Think about your players' wants, needs, and expectations.

### #20 The Lens of Pleasure
- What pleasures does your game give?
- What pleasures are missing?

### #21 The Lens of Flow
- Clear goals?
- No distractions?
- Direct feedback?
- Continuously challenging?

### #22 The Lens of Needs
- Which Maslow levels?
- Competence, autonomy, relatedness?
- How to fill needs better?

### #23 The Lens of Motivation
- What motivates players?
- Intrinsic or extrinsic?
