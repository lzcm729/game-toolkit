# Interest Curves (Jesse Schell)

From "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses" (3rd Edition)

## Core Concept

**The quality of an entertainment experience can be measured by how well its unfolding sequence of events holds a guest's interest.** Interest curves visualize this—charting interest level over time reveals the structure that separates great experiences from mediocre ones.

---

## The Good Interest Curve

```
Interest
    |     (G) Grand Finale
    |    /
    |   / (E)
    |  /  /\
    | /  /  \ (F)
    |/  / (C) \
(B)/  /         \
  /  /            \ (H)
 / (D)
(A)_________________ Time
```

**Key Points:**

| Point | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| A | Entry | Guest arrives with some initial interest |
| B | The Hook | Something that grabs attention immediately |
| C, E | Rising Peaks | Interest rising, punctuated by peaks |
| D, F | Rest Points | Brief dips allowing anticipation to build |
| G | Climax | Grand finale, most interesting moment |
| H | Resolution | Satisfying conclusion, "leave them wanting more" |

---

## The Bad Interest Curve

```
Interest
    |
    |  (b)       (d)
    |   /\       /\
    |  /  \     /  \
    | /    \   /    \
(a)/      (c)X        \(e)
  /_________________ Time
```

**Problems:**
- No hook at start
- Interest crosses **threshold** at (c)—guest gives up
- Even if something interesting happens at (d), guest already left
- No climax, just peters out

## The Hook

**The hook grabs attention immediately:**
- Musical: Opening number
- Beatles' "Revolution": Screaming guitar riff
- Hamlet: Appearance of the ghost
- Videogames: Opening cinematic

**Purpose:**
- Hint of what's to come
- Interest spike to sustain focus
- Carries guest through setup/exposition

---

## Patterns Inside Patterns

Good interest curves are **fractal**—the same pattern appears at multiple scales:

```
Level 1: Overall Game
├── Level 2: Each Chapter/World
│   ├── Level 3: Each Level
│   │   └── Level 4: Each Challenge/Encounter
```

**Example - Typical Videogame:**
1. **Overall game:** Intro → rising levels → final boss climax
2. **Each level:** New aesthetics → challenges → boss battle
3. **Each challenge:** Introduction → stepped difficulty → resolution

## Three Factors of Interest

### Factor 1: Inherent Interest

Some events are simply more interesting:
- Risk > Safety
- Fancy > Plain
- Unusual > Ordinary
- Dramatic change > Stability

**Example:** Man wrestling alligator > Man eating sandwich

### Lens #70: Inherent Interest

- What captures interest immediately?
- Does the game show something never seen/done before?
- What base instincts does it appeal to?
- What higher instincts?
- Does dramatic change and anticipation happen?

### Factor 2: Poetry of Presentation

The **aesthetics** of the experience—beautiful artistry increases interest:
- Writing quality
- Music
- Visual design
- Acting/animation
- Cinematography

### Lens #71: Beauty

- What elements make up my game? How can each be more beautiful?
- How can elements be composed in a poetic way?
- What does beauty mean in this game's context?

### Factor 3: Projection

The extent to which guests **project themselves into the experience**.

**Empathy Gradient:**
- Stranger wins lottery → Mildly interesting
- Friend wins lottery → Somewhat interesting
- You win lottery → Extremely interesting

**Key Principle:** Events that happen to us are more interesting than events that happen to others.

**Building Projection:**
- Create characters guests can empathize with
- Build consistent, immersive world
- Let guests be the main character (games' advantage)
- Create overlap between perception and imagination

### Lens #72: Projection

- What can players relate to?
- What captures imagination?
- Are there places players have always wanted to visit?
- Does player get to be a character they could imagine being?
- Are there characters players would want to meet or spy on?
- Do players get to do things they can't in real life?
- Is there an activity that once started, is hard to stop?

---

## Putting It All Together

Different factors can compensate for each other:

| Experience | Inherent Interest | Poetry | Projection |
|------------|------------------|--------|------------|
| Chainsaw juggling | HIGH | Low | Medium |
| Violin concerto | Low | HIGH | Low |
| Tetris | Low | Low | HIGH |

**Games' Advantage:** High projection can compensate for low inherent interest and poetry.

## Key Moments

### Lens #68: Moments

Disney CEO question: "What are the top ten moments in your experience?"

- What are the key moments in my game?
- How can I make each moment as powerful as possible?

**If you can't answer this question clearly, the pitch is over.**

## The Interest Curve Lens

### Lens #69: Interest Curve

- If I draw my interest curve, how is it shaped?
- Does it have a hook?
- Does interest rise gradually with rest periods?
- Is there a grand finale more interesting than everything else?
- What changes would improve the curve?
- Is there fractal structure? Should there be?
- Does my intuition match observed player interest?

---

## Practical Example: Aladdin VR

**Problem:** Experience had a flat section dragging in the middle.

**Solution Analysis:** Simply adding more interest to flat section might diminish the later climax.

**Actual Solution:** Added shortcut (merchant's tent teleport) to bypass flat section.

**Result:** Operators would whisper "go in that tent!" to players, who "seemed to have more fun."
