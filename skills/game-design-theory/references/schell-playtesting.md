# Playtesting (Jesse Schell)

From "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses" (3rd Edition)

## Core Concept

**Playtesting is your wakeup call.** It forces you to solve ugly problems you've been putting off. While terrifying—inviting people to hate your work—it is essential to making a good game. WUBALEW: When Useful, But At Least Every Week.

---

## Four Types of Testing

| Type | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Focus Groups** | Market research, audience preferences |
| **QA Testing** | Finding bugs and defects |
| **Usability Testing** | Interface clarity, learnability |
| **Playtesting** | Does it create the intended experience? |

This chapter focuses on playtesting—what designers care about most.

---

## The Emotional Reality

> "I hate playtesting."

Does it find problems early? **Yes.**
Does it build confidence? **Yes.**
Is it essential? **Yes.**
Does it fill you with terror? **Yes, yes, yes!**

**The Brutal Truth:**
Playtesting makes clear that some decisions you were completely confident about were completely wrong.

---

## Six Key Questions

### 1. WHY? (Purpose)

**Every playtest is a prototype of the game experience.**

Specific questions are better than "Is my game fun?"

**Example Questions:**
- Can players figure out the controls without a tutorial?
- Do players understand the goal of level 3?
- Is the boss fight too hard?
- Do players understand the upgrade system?
- Which character do players like most?

**Use the lenses** throughout the book to generate good playtesting questions.

### 2. WHO? (Testers)

| Type | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Developers** | Always available, deep knowledge | Too close, opinions distorted |
| **Friends/Family** | Available, comfortable talking | May be overly nice or harsh |
| **Expert Gamers** | Deep genre knowledge | May not represent target audience |
| **Tissue Testers** | Fresh eyes each time | Never see later game content |
| **Target Audience** | Most representative | Hardest to recruit |

**Tissue Testers:** Fresh playtesters you "use once and throw away"—most valuable because they can only give first impressions once.

### 3. WHEN? (Timing)

**Test at ANY stage:**

| Stage | Method |
|-------|--------|
| **Paper Prototype** | Invite playtesters to your barely-playable board game |
| **Working Prototype** | Keep testing as late into process as possible |
| **Finished Game** | Post-launch tuning, "Games as a Service" |

**WUBALEW:** When Useful, But At Least Every Week

Weekly playtesting = never going too far without feedback.

### 4. WHERE? (Location)

| Location | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **Your studio** | Convenient, all equipment available | Testers may be uncomfortable |
| **Playtesting lab** | Designed for testing, cameras, mirrors | Expensive, artificial |
| **Conventions/Events** | Varied testers, fresh eyes | Noisy, difficult to observe |
| **Tester's home** | Natural environment | Limited observation, equipment hassles |
| **Online** | Massive scale, analytics possible | Can't see faces, limited observation |

### 5. WHAT? (Observations)

**Two types of things to look for:**

1. **Things you know you're looking for:** Answers to your "why" questions
2. **Things you don't know you're looking for:** Unexpected problems and opportunities

### 6. HOW? (Methods)

#### Observation
- Watch silently (don't interrupt!)
- Note facial expressions, body language
- Note where they pause, get confused, or get excited

#### Interview
- Ask questions AFTER they play
- "How did you feel when...?"
- "What were you trying to do when...?"
- "Was there anything confusing?"

#### Survey
- Useful for quantitative data
- Note age and gender
- Don't take survey data as gospel—people make things up

#### Metrics (Online/Analytics)
- Track where players die
- Track where players quit
- Track how long players spend in each area
- A/B testing for different designs

## Observation Guidelines

**DON'T:**
- Interrupt or help
- Explain how things work
- Defend your design choices
- Hover nervously
- Tell them they're doing it wrong

**DO:**
- Watch silently
- Take detailed notes
- Note timestamps
- Look for patterns across testers
- Be grateful for criticism

---

## Interview Techniques

**Good Questions:**
- "What were you trying to do?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "What did you expect to happen?"
- "Was anything confusing?"

**Bad Questions:**
- "Wasn't that fun?" (leading)
- "Did you like X?" (yes/no)
- "What would you change?" (they're not designers)

**Listen for:**
- What they say (explicit)
- What they don't say (implicit)
- Tone of voice
- Enthusiasm or hesitation

---

## Survey Tips

1. Keep it short
2. Use rating scales (1-5, 1-10)
3. Include open-ended questions
4. Note demographics
5. Don't trust self-reported data completely

**People often don't know why they feel the way they do.**

## The Lens of Playtesting

### Lens #103: Playtesting

- Why are we doing a playtest?
- Who should test? Developers, friends, experts, tissue testers, or target audience?
- What will we look for?
- How will we get information? Observation, interview, survey, or metrics?

## Common Playtesting Mistakes

1. **Testing too late:** Test early, even with paper prototypes
2. **Testing too rarely:** WUBALEW
3. **Wrong testers:** Match testers to questions
4. **Helping too much:** Let them struggle and fail
5. **Defending the design:** Listen, don't argue
6. **Ignoring patterns:** One complaint might be noise; five is a signal
7. **Taking all feedback literally:** Understand the underlying problem
8. **Not acting on results:** If you're not going to change anything, why test?

## Playtesting at Different Stages

### Paper Prototype Stage
- Is the core loop fun?
- Are the rules understandable?
- Is the balance roughly right?

### Working Prototype Stage
- Are controls intuitive?
- Is the difficulty curve appropriate?
- Are the goals clear?

### Near-Final Stage
- Is the tutorial effective?
- Are there any showstopping bugs?
- Does the experience hold up for full play sessions?

### Post-Launch
- Where do players struggle?
- Where do players quit?
- What do players complain about?
