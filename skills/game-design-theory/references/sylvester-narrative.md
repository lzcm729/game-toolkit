# Narrative (Tynan Sylvester)

From "Designing Games: A Guide to Engineering Experiences"

## Core Challenge

**Games generate story differently from other media.** Movies/books author events directly; games design systems that generate events. This creates unique narrative challenges.

## The Desk Jumping Problem

**DESK JUMPING:** When player takes actions the character would never take because their motivations are different.

### Why It Happens

| Character Motivation | Player Motivation |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Save the princess | Entertain himself |
| Make money | See all content |
| Survive zombies | Upgrade abilities |

**Deus Ex example:** Character wants mission briefing; player dances on boss's desk because it's funny.

**GTA IV example:** Niko agonizes over killing one enemy after murdering hundreds of innocents. Player motivation (kill for fun) vs character motivation (don't kill) = nonsense.

### Solutions

| Solution | How It Works | Downside |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| **Disallow** | Block desk jumping | Players sense artificiality, stop exploring mechanics |
| **Fictional Justification** | Portal: Only character is disembodied AI, no one to desk-jump with | Limits game world |
| **Ignore** | Half-Life 2: Bullets never hit companion | Less control feeling, behavior stops quickly |
| **Incorporate** | Deus Ex: Boss scolds player for entering women's restroom | Can lead to ever-expanding scope |
| **Align Motivations** | Call of Duty 4: Combat so intense that impulse to fight overrides impulse to act stupid | **Best solution** |

> "The player's motivation doesn't have to be the same in source as their character—only in goal."

Call of Duty: Character motivated by honor/loyalty/fear; player motivated by energy/entertainment. Different sources, same goal: fight enemies hard.

## The Human Interaction Problem

**No technology can simulate human mind.** Buttons and joysticks can't express full range of human thoughts and feelings.

### Workarounds

| Method | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Avoid direct interaction** | BioShock: Sane characters only speak over radio or through glass; face-to-face characters are insane |
| **Dialogue trees** | Predefine player options and responses |
| **Reuse game verbs** | GTA IV: "Kill or spare" expressed through shoot/walk controls |
| **Use real players** | D&D: Dungeon Master plays all NPCs; social pressure motivates role-playing |

### Dialogue Tree Limitations

Player has handful of choices instead of near-infinite variety of real life. But works because designers author every side of every interaction.

## World Story

**Environmental storytelling** that doesn't require player interaction with characters.

**Fallout 3 example:**
- Scorched buildings tell history of nuclear war
- Sheet-metal shacks speak of hardscrabble life
- Character's grungy coveralls mark her as tinkerer, not socialite

**Power:** Runs continuously while player explores. Two story threads at once:
1. Backstory (this town was built by desperate people)
2. Emergent story (I walked into town and explored left)

## Emergent Story

**Stories that arise from game systems interacting with player choices.**

**Fallout 3 Super-Duper Mart example:**
- Scripted: Raiders always in same places
- Emergent: Hunters randomly appeared during fight
- Apophenia: Hunter's bravado cut short by raider bullet FEELS poignant (not modeled in mechanics)
- Combat forms mini-story with its own emotional arc

**Key insight:** Much of narrative meaning comes from **apophenia**—pattern-finding in random events. Hunter screaming last threat as he dies is random bark + combat outcome, but feels like heroic death.

### Labeling Creates Story

**Antlered raider in Fallout 3:** Not just "a raider"—he's "the weird one with goofy antlers." Label makes story construction easier.

**Improvement opportunity:** Most characters are nondescript. More identity (crazy doctor, bartender, master, slave) → better player-constructed stories.

## Goofy Undertone

**Humor serves narrative function:**
1. Lightens emotional load (purely desperate world too heavy)
2. Justifies unrealistic elements (radiation mutants)
3. Reduces impact of logical absurdities (guard says "I thought I heard something" after being shot in head)

## Fiction and Mechanics Tradeoffs

| Mechanics Can Do | Fiction Can Do |
|------------------|----------------|
| Tension | Character |
| Triumph | Plot |
| Learning | Humor |
| Social interaction | Awe, Immersion |

**They can interfere:** Game clichés (crates, amnesia, super-soldiers) exist because they justify good mechanics.

**Pinnacle of design:** Threading fiction and mechanics into single system of meaning.

## Content Ordering

**How player encounters content:**
- **Scripted:** Must pass parking lot before going inside
- **Geometric:** Must pass main room before pharmacy
- **Triggered:** Raiders enter after player explores pharmacy

**Open structure:** Player can leave/return at any time, juggle many quests. Vast number of paths through game.

## Pacing in Systemic Games

**Fallout 3 pacing:** Irregularly spiky. Tense combat → longer dialogue/exploration/healing → tense combat.

**Problem:** Carefully crafted pacing curve likely to be "sliced and diced" by easily distracted players.

**Solution:** Steady-with-spikes pacing is robust against being chopped up and reassembled.

## Setting Supports Mechanics

**Post-apocalyptic setting justifies:**
- Omnipresent violence (starving, desperate people) → repeated combat system use
- Low population density → saves designers from creating large crowds technology can't handle
- Emergent storytelling (characters shoot instead of talk) → avoids human interaction problem

## Case Study: Fallout 3 Moira Quest

**Narrative tools used:**
1. **World story:** Capital Wasteland's scorched landscape
2. **Hard-scripted:** Moira's dialogue
3. **Soft-scripted:** Combat encounters (always there, but play out differently)
4. **Emergent:** Moment-to-moment tactical decisions

**Integrated story arises from:** Interaction of scripts, game systems, and player decisions. Each player's experience is unique.

## Key Mantras

- "Games generate events; they don't author them."
- "The player's motivation doesn't have to be the same in source as their character—only in goal."
- "Apophenia lets players construct meaning from randomized elements."
- "The pinnacle of design is threading fiction and mechanics into a single system of meaning."
