# Decisions (Tynan Sylvester)

From "Designing Games: A Guide to Engineering Experiences"

## Core Thesis

**Decisions are the only emotional trigger unique to games.** Other media can provoke emotion through spectacle, character, or music; only games can do it through decision.

Understanding decisions is critical because decisions themselves come in countless shapes and sizes—important or inconsequential, difficult or easy, rushed or contemplated, packed together or generously spaced.

## Feeling the Future

**Something doesn't have to happen to generate emotions. The player need only sense the possibility of it happening.**

Standing on Empire State Building edge: petrified with fear.
Standing on friend's porch edge: at peace.
Same events occurred—only the possible futures differ.

> "In games, the player doesn't just experience what happens. His decision-making process mentally interacts with every possible outcome his mind can detect."

**Chess master staring at board:** Not idle—mentally blasting through possibility space, dancing across a thousand moves. The quality of internal decision process makes chess fascinating, not piece movement.

## Predictability

**For a decision to be meaningful, outcomes must be neither unknowable nor inevitable. They must be partially predictable.**

Without prediction → reactive thrashing, random choices
Total predictability → no mental work, no decision

**Prediction requires:**
1. **Consistent systems** - Same way in different situations
2. **Comprehensible systems** - Simple enough to understand

**Mario example:** Jump height always exactly the same. Player can look at obstacle course and know exactly which jumps work. He can envision paths and feel each one.

## Information Balance

**INFORMATION BALANCING:** Providing or denying information to make a decision comprehensible without being obvious.

### Information Starvation

Too little information makes decisions confusing and random.

**Common causes:**
- **Authored challenge preparation problem:** Game asks player to prepare for challenges before seeing them (RPG character creation at game start)
- **Fictional ambiguity:** Turkey on table—can you eat it? Sell it? Throw it to attract animals? No way to know from fiction alone

**Mass Effect 2:** 80% of players chose Soldier (most familiar class) because other options were incomprehensible at character creation.

**Useful FAQ is a warning sign:** When a text file makes a game noticeably better, that game is information-starved.

### Information Glut

Too much information erases decisions entirely. Answer already plainly stated.

**Modern Warfare 2 heartbeat sensor fix:**
- Problem: Shows all enemy locations → no thought required
- Solution 1: Doesn't work on Ninja perk enemies
- Solution 2: Only shows blips every 3.5 seconds
- Result: New classes of decisions and strategies

### Ways to Hide Information

Even complete information games have decisions because they hide information in other ways:

| Hidden In | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Future** | Chain of complex cause and effect |
| **Internal states** | Opponent's planned moves, perceived vulnerabilities |
| **Speed** | Brain takes time to process; rock-paper-scissors works because throws are simultaneous |

### Case Study: Poker Evolution

| Era | Format | Information Balance | Result |
|-----|--------|---------------------|--------|
| 1800s | 20-card, one bet | Starved | Nearly random |
| Mid-1800s | 52-card, multiple rounds | Better | Draw poker |
| Civil War | Stud poker (3 face-up) | Glut | Often obvious who wins |
| Mid-1900s | Community cards (Texas Hold'em) | Perfect | Fascinating decisions every hand |

**Key insight:** Same basic mechanics (check, bet, fold) across centuries—only information structure changed.

## Decision Scope

**DECISION SCOPE:** The amount of thought a decision takes to make.

| Scope | Time | Description | Example |
|-------|------|-------------|---------|
| **Nondecision** | 0 | So obvious it's habit | Pour milk on cereal |
| **Twitch** | <1 sec | Simple conscious reasoning | Punch or kick? |
| **Tactical** | 1-5 sec | Multiple variables considered | Which equipment to buy? |
| **Profound** | 10+ sec | Draws from broader knowledge | Chess grandmaster contemplating |
| **Impossible** | — | Beyond ability to understand | Too complex, choose randomly |

**Skill changes scope:** A profound decision for a novice is a nondecision to an expert.

**Skill ceiling defined by:** Scope of largest decisions. When largest-scoped decisions become nondecisions, player has passed the ceiling.

## Flow and Decisions

**Flow is a delicate dance of moment-to-moment decision timing.**

Imagine player's mind as small cup with hole in bottom:
- Water = Decisions
- Maintaining flow = Water always draining, cup never overflows
- Too little water → boredom (flow gap)
- Too much water → overflow (overwhelm)

### Flow Gaps

**FLOW GAP:** Period when player's mind has nothing to chew on.

**Causes:**
- Balancing delays (wizard wand 2-second cooldown with nothing else to do)
- Aesthetic elements (menu transitions, animations, dialogue)
- Stun attacks freezing victim

**Stun solutions that preserve flow:**
- Modern Warfare 2: Slow turning rate, swaying view (can still act)
- Old arcade: Scramble controls (can still act)
- Counter-Strike: White out screen (can still listen and move)

### Decision Variation

Don't feed player identical decisions at constant rate. Mix:
- Compressed twitch sequences
- Profound mind-benders with unlimited time
- Five-second tactical decisions

**Classic pacing curve:** Hook → rising action → climax → denouement

**Hard-and-fast rule:** It should vary. Neither bore with long slow periods, nor exhaust with long fast ones.

## Metagame Information

**METAGAME INFORMATION:** Information gathered from real world outside the game.

- Genre conventions (game won't be unfair)
- Friends' discussions (difficulty level)
- Studio habits (plot twist predictions)
- Technical limits (10,000 characters won't appear)

**Problem:** Player knows game will be "fair" → tension reduced, immersion weakened.

**Two solutions:**
1. **Standard:** Accept it, design systems that still work given metagame knowledge
2. **Crazy:** Call player's bluff—be unfair, be arbitrary (System Shock 2 is actually unfair, making it wonderfully immersive)

## Key Mantras

- "Decisions are the only emotional trigger that is unique to games."
- "Something doesn't have to happen to generate emotions. The player need only sense the possibility of it happening."
- "For a decision to be meaningful, its outcomes must be neither unknowable nor inevitable."
- "Good game decisions are always based around nonobvious uses of mechanics that work in obvious ways."
