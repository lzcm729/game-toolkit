# Character Design (Jesse Schell)

From "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses" (3rd Edition)

## Core Concept

**Characters bring worlds to life.** Game characters differ from those in other media—they're more physical, more fantastical, and simpler. Understanding avatars, character functions, traits, relationships, and status enables creating memorable game characters.

---

## Characters Across Media

| Aspect | Novels | Films | Games |
|--------|--------|-------|-------|
| Conflict | Mental/psychological | Emotional + physical | Almost entirely physical |
| Setting | Reality-based | Reality → Fantasy | Almost entirely fantasy |
| Complexity | Deep | Medium | Simple |

**Why Games Are Different:**
- Players do the thinking (no internal monologue)
- Characters rarely speak
- Action verbs dominate

**Opportunity:** Players want richer characters—this is challenging but valuable.

---

## Avatars

The avatar is magical—a god taking physical form. Players project themselves into avatars like they project into cars ("He hit me!" not "He hit my car!").

### First-Person vs. Third-Person

**First-person:** No visible avatar, greater direct immersion
**Third-person:** Visible avatar, power of empathy and "kinesthetic voodoo doll"

Both can work—the key is projection.

### Two Types of Effective Avatars

#### The Ideal Form
Character the player has always wanted to be:
- Mighty warrior
- Powerful wizard
- Ultra-suave secret agent
- Attractive princess

Fulfills the drive to be our best selves.

#### The Blank Slate (Iconic Character)

Less detail = more projection opportunity.

```
Detailed face → "That person"
Simple face → "A person"
Minimal face → "Could be me"
```

**Scott McCloud's Insight:** Iconic characters with detailed worlds create powerful combination. Player projects into simple avatar while being immersed in rich environment.

**Example - Mario:** Not an ideal form, but simple, hardly speaks, completely nonthreatening → easy to project into.

**Spider-Man:** Combines both—ideal form (superhero) with iconic mask (blank slate).

### Lens #85: Avatar

- Is my avatar an ideal form likely to resonate with players?
- Does my avatar have iconic qualities that let players project themselves in?

## Character Tips

### Tip #1: List Character Functions

Separate functions from characters, then match:

**Example Functions:**
1. Hero (player character)
2. Mentor (advice, items)
3. Assistant (tips)
4. Tutor (how to play)
5. Final boss
6. Minions
7. Level bosses
8. Hostage

**Creative Matching:**
- Princess Mouse as final boss instead of hostage?
- Wise Old Owl as both mentor AND final boss?
- One character fulfilling multiple functions = efficiency

### Lens #86: Character Function

- What roles do characters need to fill?
- What characters have I imagined?
- Which characters map well to which roles?
- Can any fill more than one role?
- Do I need to change characters to fit roles better?
- Do I need new characters?

### Tip #2: Define and Use Character Traits

**Bad Dialog:**
```
LESTER: Someone has stolen the king's crown!
SABU: Do you realize what this means?
LESTER: No.
```
Tells us nothing about the characters.

**Character Traits:**
- Sabu: trustworthy, short tempered, valiant, fiery lover
- Lester: arrogant, sarcastic, spiritual, impulsive

**Better Dialog:**
```
LESTER (exploding into room): By the Gods! Sabu, I have news!
  → impulsive, spiritual

SABU (covering herself): How dare you intrude on my privacy!
  → short tempered

LESTER: Whatever. Maybe you don't care that the crown was stolen?
  → arrogant, sarcastic
```

**Apply to Actions Too:**
- Does "sneaky" show in jump animation?
- Should a depressed character only walk, not run?

### Lens #87: Character Traits

- What traits define my character?
- How do these manifest in words, actions, and appearance?

### Tip #3: Use the Interpersonal Circumplex

Two axes: **Friendliness** and **Dominance**

```
        Dominant
            |
    Hostile ---+--- Friendly
            |
        Submissive
```

Map characters relative to a central character. Notice:
- Extreme positions = interesting characters
- Most-interacted characters closest on graph
- Empty quadrants reveal character gaps

**Example - Han Solo:**
- Darth Vader: Hostile, Dominant
- Chewbacca: Friendly, slightly Submissive
- Princess Leia: Friendly, slightly Dominant
- C-3PO: Friendly, very Submissive

### Lens #88: Interpersonal Circumplex

- Are there gaps in the chart? Should they be filled?
- Are there extreme characters? Should there be?
- Are friends in same or different quadrants?

### Tip #4: Make a Character Web

List how each character feels about every other character:

**Example - Archie Comics:**

**Archie about Veronica:** Lured by elegance and beauty
**Archie about Betty:** True love, but her insecurity gives mixed signals
**Archie about Jughead:** Best friend, both underdogs

**Veronica about Archie:** Attractive, sometimes dates him to frustrate Betty
**Veronica about Betty:** Childhood friend, but frustrated Betty is a better person

**Questions it raises:**
- Any connections unaccounted for?
- Too many similar connections?

### Lens #89: Character Web

- How does each character feel about each other?
- Are there unaccounted connections?
- Are connections too similar?

### Tip #5: Use Status

**Status is what you do, not who you are.**

Whenever people interact, there's hidden negotiation: Who is in charge?

**Low-Status Behaviors:**
- Fidgeting
- Avoiding eye contact
- Touching own face
- Generally tense

**High-Status Behaviors:**
- Relaxed, in control
- Strong eye contact
- NOT moving head while speaking
- Taking up space

**Status is Relative:**
Darth Vader is high-status with Leia, low-status with the Emperor.

**Status in Games:**
*Munch's Oddysee*: Slave (low status) facing arrogant enemies (high status), helped by slavish followers (low status). Comedy from unexpected status reversals.

**Unexplored Opportunity:**
Most game characters behave the same no matter who's around. Next generation: characters aware of status who automatically adopt appropriate behaviors.

### Lens #90: Status

- What is the status of each character?
- How do they show status through behavior?
- Do status conflicts arise? How are they resolved?
- Do characters change status during the game?

## Character Transformation

Great stories often involve the protagonist transforming to overcome obstacles.

**Questions:**
- How does your character change?
- What causes the transformation?
- Is the transformation visible to the player?

---

## Status and Interest

Things that change dramatically are inherently interesting. Status seesaws during arguments—people vie to raise their own or lower opponents' status.

---

## Key Mantras

> "The less detail, the more projection."

> "Status is something you do, not who you are."

> "If characters seem unaware of each other, they seem less alive."
